# EP02 — Conversation: Randi Salvato / Aiden Jae

## Format
Two people on camera. Riverside split screen. Conversational, not interview.

## Runtime
40-60 minutes. Cut when it's done.

## Context
Randi is your wife. She studied oil painting at Parsons and Brooklyn College. She designs fine jewelry manufactured from 100% recycled solid 9k gold through RJC-certified partners in Bangkok (Beauty Gems). You built the entire brand system: identity, photography, Shopify platform, packaging concepts. This is a real case study of building a brand from zero, and you both lived it.

This is not a husband-and-wife show. This is two practitioners who collaborated on a real project talking about what happened.

---

## Cold Open (you, on camera, before Randi appears)

I built my wife's jewelry brand from scratch. Identity, photography, Shopify, packaging. She's the designer and merchandiser. I'm the systems person. This is what actually happened.

## Opening Question
Start here, directed at Randi:

**"What did you have before any of the brand work started? What was the business?"**

Let her describe it. The product existed. The expertise existed. There was no way to put it in front of anyone. No brand, no platform, no photography system.

## Thread 1 — What Makes the Brand Different (~10 min)
The first question you had to answer before building anything:

- What makes Aiden Jae different from every other jewelry brand?
- The answer: recycled solid gold, genuine stones, sustainable sourcing, in-house wool felt pouches, pollinator donation. All of it real, none of it marketing.
- Ask Randi: **"How much of that was intentional positioning vs. just how you wanted to run the business?"**
- The point: the brand story was already there in how she operates. The work was making it visible, not inventing it.

## Thread 2 — Photography as Communication (~10 min)
You did all the photography. This is where a brand like this proves itself or lies.

- Talk about the approach: honest light, no retouching that hides the hand-finishing, no color grading that promises something the piece doesn't deliver.
- Top-down for primary, angled for secondary, detail for third. Aspect ratios change because each piece has different visual weight.
- Ask Randi: **"When you look at the product photos, do they show what the pieces actually look like?"**
- The point: the photography does most of the communication. When you see recycled gold texture under honest light, you're seeing the quality and the sourcing story without reading a word.

## Thread 3 — The Platform Problem (~10 min)
A generic Shopify template treats every product the same. A quality ring in that grid looks like a commodity.

- Talk about what you built: custom Liquid templates, SCSS framework, product page architecture.
- Each product page shows material story, production notes, design rationale alongside the piece.
- Ask Randi: **"What was the difference when you saw the products in the custom system vs. the default template?"**
- The point: the platform either reinforces the brand or overrides it. Most small businesses never realize the template is undermining them.

## Thread 4 — Working Together (~10 min)
This is the human part.

- You're married. You collaborated on a business. That's a specific dynamic.
- Ask Randi: **"What was hard about this? What did we fight about?"**
- Be honest about the tension between systems thinking and creative instinct.
- The point: building a brand for someone you know deeply is different from building one for a client. You have context a hired designer never would. You also have no professional distance.

## Thread 5 — What Held and What Didn't (~5 min)
The brand has been live. What proved out?

- Ask Randi: **"What part of the system works the way we intended? What doesn't?"**
- New collections (Banyan, Knotted Tropics, Sunrise/Sunset, Star Light/Star Bright) all hold the brand logic because the system is built on principles that flex.
- The point: the test of a brand system is whether it survives new work. Not whether it looks good on launch day.

## Close
No formal wrap-up. When the conversation reaches a natural end, stop. If there's a final thought, say it. If not, just end.
